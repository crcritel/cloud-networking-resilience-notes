# Writing Standards

This document defines the tone, style, and language rules for all content in this repository.

## Tone

Precise. Analytical. Non-hyped. Every sentence must earn its place. Write as if the reader is a peer architect reviewing your design document during an architecture review.

## Language Rules

### Avoid Buzzwords

Do not use terms like "game-changing," "revolutionary," "next-generation," "cutting-edge," or "best-in-class." These terms carry no technical meaning and erode credibility.

### Avoid Marketing Phrasing

This is not a product page. Do not write to persuade. Write to inform and analyze. Sentences like "This powerful approach enables teams to..." belong in sales collateral, not here.

### No Emojis

Never. Under any circumstances.

### No Casual Language

Avoid colloquialisms, slang, and informal phrasing. "Basically," "just," "simply," and "obviously" are banned. If something were obvious, it would not need an article.

### Prefer Short Declarative Sentences

Long compound sentences obscure meaning. Break complex ideas into sequential short statements. Each sentence should convey one idea.

### Always Include Trade-offs

Every recommendation, every pattern, every architectural choice has a cost. State it. If you cannot articulate the trade-off, you do not understand the pattern well enough to write about it.

### Always Include Operational Implications

Architecture does not exist in a vacuum. Every pattern has operational consequences: monitoring requirements, deployment complexity, on-call burden, debugging difficulty. Address them.

## Structural Integrity

### Arguments Must Be Supported

Do not make claims without supporting them. If you state that a pattern reduces blast radius, explain the mechanism. If you claim a trade-off exists, quantify it where possible.

### Diagrams Must Be Referenced

Every diagram included in an article must be explicitly referenced and walked through in the text. A diagram without a walkthrough is incomplete.

### References Must Be Credible

Cite primary sources where possible: RFCs, published research, official documentation. Blog posts and opinion pieces are acceptable only as supplementary references.
